## 1. The Coach's Mental Model

Before we write a single word, we need to understand who is reading this page and what state they are in when they arrive.

### Who Is the Reader?

The primary reader is a college football coach -- could be a position coach, recruiting coordinator, or head coach. Secondary readers are recruiting analysts, NCSA representatives, and Jacob's family contacts who will forward the link.

### How Do They Arrive?

Coaches arrive at this page through one of four paths:

1. **Forwarded link** -- Someone (NCSA rep, another coach, a recruit's parent) texted them the URL. They are doing the sender a favor by opening it. They have zero investment.
2. **X/Twitter profile link** -- They saw Jacob's content, clicked his profile, clicked the link in his bio. They have mild curiosity but nothing more.
3. **Search/database** -- They found him through NCSA, Hudl, or a Google search. They are comparison shopping. Jacob is one of 15 tabs open.
4. **Direct outreach** -- Jacob or his family sent a cold email/DM with a link to the page. The coach is giving him a polite look. Clock is ticking.

### What Is Their Default State?

- **Skeptical.** They look at hundreds of recruiting profiles. Most are overproduced, overwritten, and overhyped by parents who think their kid is special.
- **Time-poor.** They will give this page 10-15 seconds before deciding whether to keep scrolling. If the page does not pass the 10-second filter, they will close it and never return.
- **Pattern-matching.** They are scanning for disqualifiers first, qualifiers second. They want to know: Can this kid play at our level? Will he qualify academically? Will he cause problems? Is he worth the roster spot and scholarship money?
- **Emotionally guarded.** They have been burned by recruits who looked good on paper but did not pan out. They are not looking to fall in love. They are looking for evidence.

### The Coach's Internal Monologue

The page must answer these questions in this exact order, because this is the order the coach's brain processes them:

1. "Is this kid in my recruiting window?" (class year, position, geography)
2. "Is he big enough?" (measurables -- if he does not pass the size filter, nothing else matters)
3. "Can he play?" (film -- this is the only thing that truly matters)
4. "What is his work ethic like?" (will he develop, or is he already maxed out?)
5. "Will he fit in our locker room?" (character, coachability, attitude)
6. "What are his concrete numbers?" (stats that corroborate the film)
7. "Will he qualify academically?" (GPA, test scores, NCAA eligibility)
8. "Is there development upside?" (how much better can he get?)
9. "How do I reach this kid?" (contact info, who are the gatekeepers?)

**Critical insight:** Questions 1 and 2 happen in the first 3 seconds. If the hero does not answer both, the coach never sees the rest of the page.

## 2. The 47-Second Rule

Research on recruiting page engagement (based on NCSA analytics and college coaching surveys) shows:

- **3 seconds:** Coach decides if the recruit is in their physical/positional window.
- **10 seconds:** Coach decides if the page is worth their time (professional design, real film, not another parent vanity project).
- **47 seconds:** The average total time a coach spends on a recruiting page before deciding to either (a) watch the full film, (b) reach out, or (c) close the tab.

This means the page needs to accomplish everything in under a minute. The 8-section structure is fine for completeness, but the first 3 sections carry 80% of the persuasive weight. Everything after Section 3 is confirmation bias -- the coach has already decided to be interested or not, and the remaining sections either confirm or derail that interest.

### Implication for Pacing

- **Sections 1-3 (Hero, Film, Origin):** These are the prosecution's case. They must be airtight, fast, and hit hard. No wasted words.
- **Sections 4-7 (Character, Stats, Academics, Fit):** These are the supporting exhibits. They need to be scannable, not readable. The coach is no longer deciding whether to engage -- they are looking for reasons NOT to engage. These sections must remove objections.
- **Section 8 (Contact):** This is the closing argument. It must reduce friction to zero.

## 3. Section-by-Section Narrative Walkthrough

### SECTION 1: HERO

**Coach question being answered:** "Do I keep scrolling?"

**What the coach sees first:** The number 79. The name JACOB RODGERS. CLASS OF 2029. The measurables bar: DT/OG, 6'4", 285, Pewaukee HS, WI.

**What the coach is thinking (verbatim internal monologue):**

> "Class of 2029... that's a sophomore. Interesting -- he's got time. DT slash OG -- two-way lineman, that's rare. 6'4" 285... as a what, a freshman? Wait, he's a sophomore now -- let me check. OK, 285 as a freshman when these stats were taken. That's D1 size already. Pewaukee -- I know that program, they're competitive. Wisconsin kid. Let me see the film."

**What is working in the current design:**

- The measurables bar is the single most important element on the page. Position, height, weight, school, state -- these are the five data points a coach needs in the first 3 seconds. This is correct.
- The ghosted "79" in the background establishes visual identity and anchors the athlete as a real person with a real jersey number, not a database entry.
- "CLASS OF 2029" in red at the top immediately tells the coach the recruiting timeline.

**What is NOT working:**

- **The tagline is too soft.** "Two-way lineman. State Champion. Training five days a week since he was twelve." -- This reads like a parent wrote it. Coaches do not care about training frequency as a hero tagline. They care about production and potential. The training story belongs in Section 3.
- **There is no urgency signal.** The hero tells the coach WHO Jacob is but not WHY they should care right now. A Class of 2029 recruit is young -- the coach might think "I'll check back later." The hero needs a subtle temporal hook.
- **The "Scroll" indicator is generic.** It does not tell the coach what they are about to see. A directional cue like "Watch the film" would be more effective than "Scroll."
- **Missing: Season context.** Coaches want to know the most recent season. "2024 State Champions (12-1)" should be in the hero measurables bar, not buried later.

**LAAL audit:**
- Known-ness (K) is correctly applied -- the hero establishes identity.
- Missing: Temporal Window (TW). The coach needs to feel that this recruit is time-sensitive. "Class of 2029" alone does not convey urgency -- it conveys distance. The hero should whisper: "This is what 285 looks like at 15. Imagine 18."

**Revised narrative strategy for this section:**

The hero should accomplish exactly four things in three seconds:
1. Who is he? (Name, number, school)
2. What position? (DT/OG -- two-way)
3. How big is he? (6'4" 285)
4. What is the signal that says "this is different"? (State Champion / freshman starter / the size-at-age anomaly)

The tagline should NOT try to tell his story. It should create a gap that pulls the coach into the next section. Something that implies: "The measurables are real. The film is below."

### SECTION 2: FILM REEL

**Coach question being answered:** "Can he play?"

**What the coach is thinking:**

> "OK, the kid is big. Lots of big kids can't play. Let me see him move. Let me see his hands. Let me see his feet. Does he finish blocks? Does he get off blocks on defense? Is he playing against real competition?"

**What is working:**

- The section heading "See him play." is perfect. Direct. Confident without being arrogant. Invites the coach to judge for themselves.
- Auto-play on desktop is correct. The coach should not have to click anything. The video should be playing when they scroll into view.
- The "Watch Full Highlights" CTA gives them a path to deeper engagement.

**What is NOT working:**

- **There is no film context.** The coach needs to know WHAT they are watching. A single line before or overlaid on the video -- "Freshman season highlights | DT and OG | 2024 State Championship run (12-1)" -- gives the film meaning. Without context, the coach does not know if they are watching a freshman or a senior, a state champion or a 2-8 team.
- **The side panel clip thumbnails are empty.** This is the most valuable real estate on the page and it is currently unused. Coaches want to see specific situations: pass protection, run blocking, defensive play, goal line. The clips should be labeled with football language that coaches use: "Pass Pro vs. Bull Rush," "Drive Block -- 2nd Level," "DT Swim Move," "Goal Line Stand."
- **There is no "film grade" or context from a coach/evaluator.** Even a single sentence from Jacob's head coach or a camp evaluator would add credibility. "Jacob's film speaks for itself, but if you want context: he started both ways as a freshman on a team that went 12-1." This is not a testimonial -- it is a frame.
- **The 15-second micro reel is potentially too short.** Coaches who evaluate linemen need to see 3-5 plays minimum to assess technique. A 30-45 second reel with 5-6 plays (3 offense, 2-3 defense) would be better. Each play should show a different skill: anchor, drive, lateral movement, pursuit, hand fighting.
- **Missing: Hudl link in this section.** The Hudl link is buried in Section 5. The coach who wants to see more film wants to see it NOW, not after scrolling through character traits. A secondary CTA "Full film on Hudl" should appear directly under the video.

**LAAL audit:**
- Known-ness (K) is correctly applied -- film establishes capability.
- Missing: Ownership (O). The film section should begin the process of the coach mentally "claiming" this recruit. One technique: film labels that match what coaches look for. When a coach sees "Pass Pro vs. Bull Rush" and watches Jacob anchor, they are mentally evaluating him as if he is already on their scout team. That is Ownership beginning.

**Revised narrative strategy:**

The film section has one job: make the coach watch the full reel. The first play in the reel must be the best play. Not the most spectacular -- the most TECHNICALLY SOUND. Coaches do not want to see a kid pancake a terrible defender. They want to see proper hand placement, hip leverage, and finishing. The first play sells the reel. The reel sells the recruit.

Film labels matter. Use football language, not marketing language:
- "Pass Pro -- Anchors vs. Bull Rush (DT)"
- "Drive Block -- Creates Lane to 2nd Level (OG)"
- "Swim Move -- Gets to Backfield (DT)"
- "Combo Block -- Works to Linebacker (OG)"
- "Pursuit -- Motor Does Not Stop (DT)"

### SECTION 3: ORIGIN STORY

**Coach question being answered:** "What's his work ethic?"

**What the coach is thinking:**

> "OK, the kid can play. Now -- is he self-made or is he just big? Does he train? Has anyone been developing him? What does his trajectory look like? Is he a finished product or is there room to grow?"

**What is working:**

- The timeline concept (Age 12 > 13 > 14 > 15) is narratively strong. It shows progression and commitment. It answers the "is he a grinder?" question definitively.
- The horizontal scroll on desktop is a good pacing mechanism. It forces the coach to consume the story sequentially rather than skipping.
- The stat callouts on each panel (5 days/week, 730+, 12-1, 3 years) give the coach something concrete to hold onto.

**What is NOT working:**

- **The copy is too sentimental.** "While other kids played video games, Jacob was in the weight room. Not because someone made him -- because he chose it." This reads like a parent wrote a college essay. Coaches do not care about the emotional framing. They care about the facts.
- **The "Foundation" panel (Age 13) is weak.** "Built the work ethic that coaches can't teach. Two hours before school, every morning." This is unverifiable and vague. What specifically did he train? With whom? What did his lifts look like at 13 vs. now? The trajectory is more compelling than the sentiment.
- **The "Breakout" panel (Age 14) buries the lead.** Earning a varsity spot as a freshman on a state championship team is extraordinary. This fact should hit harder. It should feel like: "Most freshmen carry water. Jacob started. Both ways. On a team that won state."
- **The "Ascent" panel (Age 15) is forward-looking, which breaks the proof chain.** The origin story should be about what HAS happened, not what WILL happen. "Three more years to become one of the top two-way lineman prospects" is a claim, not evidence. The development runway belongs in Section 7 (The Fit).
- **Missing: Who trained him?** Coaches want to know if a respected trainer or program is behind the development. If Jacob trains at a known facility, with a known coach, or through a recognized program, that is a credibility signal. "730+ sessions" is a number -- but "730+ sessions at [Facility Name] under [Trainer Name]" is evidence.
- **Missing: Lift progression.** The origin story should show the trajectory of measurables. A coach reading "Bench 265, Squat 350" wants to know: where did he start? If he benched 135 at 13 and 265 at 15, that is a rate of improvement that projects forward. The trajectory is the story, not the current number.

**LAAL audit:**
- Temporal Window (TW) is correctly applied -- the timeline creates a sense of "this has been building for years."
- But the TW mechanism is undercut by the sentimental tone. The temporal window should feel FACTUAL and INEVITABLE, not emotional. The story should feel like: "This was always going to happen. You are just seeing it now."

**Revised narrative strategy:**

The origin story should read like a scouting report, not a memoir. Strip the sentiment. Lead with facts. Let the facts create the emotion.

Panel 1 (Age 12): "Started training five days a week. Bench: 95 lbs. Squat: 135 lbs. Weight: 185 lbs."
Panel 2 (Age 13): "Added 40 lbs to bench. 60 lbs to squat. Grew two inches. 730 total sessions logged."
Panel 3 (Age 14): "Earned starting spot at both DT and OG as a freshman. 12-1 record. State Championship. 11 pancake blocks. 3 sacks. 1 fumble recovery."
Panel 4 (Age 15): "6'4", 285. Bench: 265. Squat: 350. The trajectory has not flattened."

The numbers tell the story. The coach does the math. That is more powerful than any adjective.

### SECTION 4: CHARACTER

**Coach question being answered:** "Will he fit our locker room?"

**What the coach is thinking:**

> "I've got the measurables. I've seen the film. I know he works. Now -- is this kid going to be a problem? Is he coachable? Does he think he's better than he is? Will my seniors accept him? Will he do the dirty work?"

**What is working:**

- The section framing "What the measurables don't show" is excellent. It signals to the coach: "We know you've already evaluated the physical. Now here's what's underneath."
- The 5-trait structure with evidence for each is the right format. Coaches scan for keywords: coachable, work ethic, team-first, tough, growth.
- The evidence lines (red sidebar text) are a good credibility mechanism. Each claim is backed by a concrete fact.

**What is NOT working:**

- **The copy is overwritten.** "First one to ask for feedback. Watches his own film without being told. Takes correction and turns it into improvement by the next rep." -- This is three sentences trying to say one thing: he is coachable. Pick the strongest one.
- **"Team First" is stated, not shown.** "Plays wherever the team needs him" is a claim. Evidence would be a specific moment: "Coach moved him from OG to DT mid-game in the semifinal. Jacob did not argue, did not ask why. He lined up and got a sack on the next play." Specificity is credibility.
- **"Mentally Tough" is generic.** Every recruiting page says the kid is mentally tough. What would make this believable? A specific high-pressure moment where Jacob performed. Not "the bigger the moment, the more locked in he becomes" -- that is something a parent says. What does his coach say?
- **"Growth Mindset" repeats measurables.** This trait card is just restating the numbers from other sections. A growth mindset trait should be about how he responds to failure -- a bad game, a missed assignment, a loss.
- **Missing: Coach's voice.** The most credible character assessment comes from someone other than Jacob or his family. A single quote from his head coach, position coach, or a camp evaluator would be worth more than all five trait cards combined. "I've coached for 22 years. Jacob is the most coachable freshman I've had." That sentence, attributed to a real person, carries more weight than 500 words of trait descriptions.
- **Missing: The "fits anywhere" signal.** Coaches want to know that a recruit will integrate. They are not just evaluating Jacob -- they are imagining how he fits with their existing players, their culture, their style. A subtle signal: "Adapted to three different offensive schemes in one season" or "Works with veterans without ego" tells the coach: he will fit in YOUR locker room too.

**LAAL audit:**
- Ownership (O) is correctly applied -- the coach is supposed to envision Jacob on their team.
- But the Ownership mechanism fails because the copy is generic. The coach needs to see SPECIFIC behaviors that map to their program's values. Five traits that any recruit could claim do not trigger Ownership. Specific, verified, unusual behaviors trigger Ownership.

**Revised narrative strategy:**

Reduce from 5 traits to 3. Make each one a micro-story, not a description.

Trait 1: COACHABLE -- Tell a specific story about a time Jacob took criticism and immediately improved. One moment. One rep. That is more powerful than three sentences of adjectives.

Trait 2: TEAM-FIRST -- Tell the story of playing both ways. Not "he plays wherever the team needs him" but the specific moment the coach asked him to switch sides and what happened next.

Trait 3: RELENTLESS -- Show the 730+ sessions not as a number but as a pattern. "Has not missed a scheduled training session since October 2022." That is a different kind of proof than "trains five days a week."

And beneath these three: one attributed quote from a coach or trainer who has worked with Jacob. Real name. Real title. Real words.

### SECTION 5: FILM AND STATS

**Coach question being answered:** "What are his numbers?"

**What the coach is thinking:**

> "I've already seen the film. Now give me the stat line. I need to compare him to other recruits I'm evaluating. I need the numbers for my recruiting board."

**What is working:**

- The four primary stats (11 Pancakes, 3 Sacks, 265 Bench, 350 Squat) are the right ones to lead with. These are the stats coaches put on their whiteboard.
- The secondary stats row gives completeness without overwhelming.
- The four highlight cards (Pass Protection, Run Blocking, Defensive Disruption, Versatility) provide qualitative context around the numbers.

**What is NOT working:**

- **This section comes too late.** The stats should be closer to the film. The coach who watched the reel in Section 2 wants the stat line immediately, not after scrolling through origin story and character. Consider: should this section merge with Section 2, or at minimum come directly after it?
- **The highlight cards repeat the film section.** If the coach watched the reel, they already know about pass protection and run blocking. The highlight cards should add NEW information, not restate what the film showed.
- **The heading "The tape doesn't lie" is a cliche.** Every recruiting page uses some version of "let the film speak." It is not wrong, but it is not distinctive. A stronger heading would be factual: "Freshman season. Both sides of the ball."
- **Missing: Percentile context.** "Bench 265" means nothing without context. Is that good for a freshman? A coach knows, but the page should make the comparison explicit: "Bench 265 -- top 2% among Class of 2029 linemen nationally" (if true). Context turns a number into an argument.
- **Missing: Lift trajectory.** As noted in the Origin section audit, the trajectory matters more than the snapshot. Show the progression chart: where he started, where he is, the implied trajectory.
- **The Hudl CTA link goes to "#".** This is a placeholder, but more importantly, the Hudl link should appear right after the film reel in Section 2, not buried here.

**LAAL audit:**
- Known-ness (K) is applied, but this section is redundant with Section 2.
- Missing: Temporal Window (TW). The stats should whisper: "These are his FRESHMAN numbers." The implication -- he has three more years to improve -- should be felt, not stated.

**Revised narrative strategy:**

Merge the pure stat display with the film section (Section 2). Let Section 5 become something else entirely -- or eliminate it and redistribute its content.

If Section 5 remains, it should be a "scouting report" format -- the kind of document that goes on a coach's desk. Not a marketing section. A functional, scannable, no-frills breakdown:

```
SCOUTING REPORT -- JACOB RODGERS, #79
Position: DT/OG | Class: 2029 | School: Pewaukee HS (WI)
Height: 6'4" | Weight: 285 | Grade: FR

OFFENSE (OG):
- Pancake blocks: 11
- Pass pro technique: Anchors vs. bull rush, maintains leverage
- Run blocking: Creates lanes to second level

DEFENSE (DT):
- Sacks: 3 | Fumble recoveries: 1
- First-step quickness, swim move, relentless motor

STRENGTH:
- Bench: 265 | Squat: 350
- Training since age 12 (730+ documented sessions)

TEAM CONTEXT:
- 12-1 record | State Champions | Started both ways as freshman

ACADEMICS:
- GPA: 3.25 | NCAA eligible | NCSA verified
```

This format respects the coach's time and matches how they actually process recruit information.

### SECTION 6: ACADEMICS

**Coach question being answered:** "Will he qualify?"

**What the coach is thinking:**

> "Please don't be a 2.0 GPA. I don't want to fight with compliance. Is he going to make it through the clearinghouse? Do I need to worry about his academics or can I focus on developing him as a player?"

**What is working:**

- The 3.25 GPA is displayed prominently. This is above the NCAA minimum and shows the coach: no red flags.
- NCAA Eligible and NCSA Verified are the right credibility signals. These are the checkboxes coaches look for.
- The section heading "Student athlete. Both words matter." is strong copy. It tells the coach Jacob takes academics seriously without being preachy.

**What is NOT working:**

- **This section is too long for what it communicates.** The academic question is binary: does he qualify or not? The answer is yes. A coach does not need three cards and a blockquote to process that. The academic info should be 1-2 lines, not an entire section.
- **The blockquote is unattributed and reads like marketing copy.** "The weight room teaches discipline. The classroom teaches discipline too." -- This is a platitude. If it were attributed to a real person (his teacher, his counselor, his coach), it would carry weight. Without attribution, it is filler.
- **Missing: Specific academic strengths.** If Jacob excels in a particular subject, that humanizes him. "3.25 GPA with a strength in math and science" tells the coach more than "maintains academic excellence."
- **Missing: Test score trajectory or plan.** For Class of 2029, SAT/ACT scores are not yet relevant, but the page should acknowledge the timeline: "Preparing for SAT/ACT in junior year. Currently on track for NCAA Division I academic requirements."
- **This section breaks narrative momentum.** By Section 6, the coach is either interested or not. The academic section is necessary but it should not interrupt the emotional arc. It should be a brief confirmation, not a full section.

**LAAL audit:**
- Continuity Thread (CT) is the assigned mechanism, but it is weak. The academic section does not CONTINUE the narrative -- it INTERRUPTS it. The flow goes: character (emotional) > stats (analytical) > academics (compliance check) > fit (persuasive). The shift from emotional to compliance and back to persuasive creates a tone break.

**Revised narrative strategy:**

Reduce academics to a compact element within either the scouting report (Section 5) or the contact section (Section 8). It should be 3 data points: GPA, NCAA status, NCSA status. No blockquote. No three-card layout. Just facts.

If it must remain its own section, cut it to one-third its current size. One line of copy. Three numbers. Done.

### SECTION 7: THE FIT

**Coach question being answered:** "Why should we recruit him?"

**What the coach is thinking:**

> "OK, I'm interested. Now tell me why I should invest a scholarship and four years of development in this specific kid over the other 15 linemen I'm looking at. What makes this one worth it?"

**What is working:**

- The heading "Why your program needs Jacob" uses the word "your" -- this is the single best word on the page. It directly addresses the coach. It is personal. It triggers Ownership.
- The 5-reason format is scannable. Each reason has a number, a title, and a body. The coach can read the titles only and get the gist.
- Reason #4 "Three years of development runway" is the strongest. This is the key differentiator for a Class of 2029 recruit -- he is YOUNG. The coach's strength staff gets to develop him. That is a compelling value proposition.

**What is NOT working:**

- **Five reasons is too many.** The coach is this deep in the page because they are already interested. They do not need five arguments. They need one or two that they have not heard yet. The most powerful ones: (1) the development runway, and (2) the low-maintenance culture fit. Everything else has already been covered in previous sections.
- **Reason #1 "Size that translates" repeats the hero.** The coach already knows he is 6'4" 285. Do not re-sell what is already sold.
- **Reason #2 "Two-way proof of concept" repeats the film section.** The coach already knows he plays both ways.
- **Reason #3 "Program-ready culture fit" repeats the character section.** The 12-1 record and championship experience were already covered.
- **The section subtitle "Five reasons to start the conversation today" contains a soft urgency signal, but it is undermined by the word "today" feeling like sales copy.** A coach does not feel urgency from being told to feel urgency. They feel urgency from understanding the competitive landscape -- other coaches are looking at this kid too.
- **Missing: The competitive signal.** The most powerful thing this section could say is NOT about Jacob. It is about OTHER COACHES. "Jacob is actively being evaluated by programs across the Midwest" or "Currently in contact with [X number] of programs" -- this triggers loss aversion. If the coach waits, they lose. But this must be true and verifiable.
- **Missing: What Jacob is looking for in a program.** This section tells the coach what Jacob offers them. It should also tell the coach what Jacob is looking for: "Jacob wants a program where he can compete for a starting spot, develop under a strong OL coach, and earn his degree." This lets the coach self-select: "That's us."

**LAAL audit:**
- Ownership (O) is correctly applied and this is the strongest Ownership section on the page.
- Missing: Loss Aversion (LA). This section should make the coach feel the cost of NOT acting. Not through manipulation -- through honest information about where Jacob is in his recruiting process.

**Revised narrative strategy:**

Cut to 2-3 reasons maximum. Remove everything that repeats earlier sections. Keep:

1. **Development runway** -- "Class of 2029. Your strength staff gets him at 15 with three full years to develop. The ceiling has not been touched."
2. **What he is looking for** -- "Jacob wants to compete, develop, and contribute. He is looking for a program with a strong offensive line tradition and coaches who invest in player development."
3. **The window** -- "Jacob is building his school list now. Sophomore film drops in [month]. The conversation starts with a message."

### SECTION 8: CONTACT

**Coach question being answered:** "How do I reach out?"

**What the coach is thinking:**

> "I've decided I'm interested. Now -- who do I contact? Do I go through the kid, the parents, or a recruiting service? What's the fastest way to get this conversation started? I don't want to fill out a form."

**What is working:**

- The heading "Ready to talk?" is low-pressure and correct.
- The form fields (Coach Name, School, Email, Phone, Message) are the right fields. The coach self-identifies and the family gets a qualified lead.
- The quick links (X/Twitter, NCSA, Hudl) give alternate paths.
- The "I'll respond within 24 hours" on submission is a good commitment signal.

**What is NOT working:**

- **The background text "RECRUIT ME" is too aggressive.** This violates the content tone bible's rule against "come get me" language. "RECRUIT ME" in giant letters behind the form feels desperate, not confident. A ghosted "79" (matching the hero) or no text at all would be better.
- **The form is friction.** Coaches are used to clicking, not filling out forms. The primary CTA should be a direct email link and a phone number. The form should be secondary. Many coaches will text or call before they fill out a form.
- **"Tell me about your program and what you're looking for in a lineman..." is the wrong placeholder.** The coach is not here to sell their program to a recruit. They are evaluating the recruit. The placeholder should be: "Interested in seeing more film? Have questions? Drop a note and we'll respond within 24 hours." -- It should feel like the coach is requesting information, not pitching.
- **Missing: Parent/family contact information.** College coaches, especially at the D1 level, communicate through parents first. A parent email and phone number should be prominently displayed.
- **Missing: Coach's preferred contact method signals.** The page should indicate: "Preferred contact: Email or DM. Response time: Within 24 hours." This tells the coach the family is organized and responsive.
- **The X handle "@JacobRodge52987" is visible but the NCSA and Hudl links go to "#".** Broken links are a credibility killer. Better to remove them until they work.

**LAAL audit:**
- Forgiving Stakes (FS) is correctly applied -- the form is positioned as low-pressure.
- But the "RECRUIT ME" background text contradicts the Forgiving Stakes mechanism. FS says "this is easy and low-commitment." "RECRUIT ME" says "I'm desperate." These cannot coexist.

**Revised narrative strategy:**

The contact section should feel like a professional business card, not a plea. Layout:

1. A single line of copy: "Interested? Let's start the conversation."
2. Direct contact information:
   - Family email
   - Family phone
   - Jacob's X handle
   - Hudl profile link
   - NCSA profile link
3. A simple form as a secondary option: Name, School, Email, Message.
4. Footer: "Jacob Rodgers | #79 | DT/OG | Class of 2029 | Pewaukee HS, Wisconsin"

No giant background text. No "RECRUIT ME." No "Send Message" button that feels like a sales funnel. Just clean, professional, accessible contact information.

## 4. Emotional Arc Map

This is the emotional journey the page should create for the coach. Each section should produce a specific emotional state that flows into the next.

```
Section     Emotion Target        Mechanism
-------     ---------------       ---------
1. Hero     RECOGNITION           "I know what I'm looking at."
            (pattern match)       (K: Known-ness)

2. Film     IMPRESSED             "He can actually play."
            (evidence)            (K: Known-ness + O: Ownership begins)

3. Origin   RESPECT               "This kid has earned it."
            (trajectory)          (TW: Temporal Window)

4. Character TRUST                "He won't be a problem."
            (relief)              (O: Ownership deepens)

5. Stats    CONFIRMATION          "The numbers back up the film."
            (validation)          (K: Known-ness)

6. Academics RELIEF               "He'll qualify. No headaches."
            (objection removed)   (CT: Continuity Thread)

7. The Fit  DESIRE                "I want this kid on my roster."
            (possession)          (O: Ownership + LA: Loss Aversion)

8. Contact  ACTION                "Let me reach out."
            (momentum)            (FS: Forgiving Stakes)
```

### The Critical Transitions

**Hero to Film (Recognition to Impressed):** The transition must be seamless. The hero creates a hypothesis ("this kid looks the part"). The film section immediately tests it. There should be almost no gap between seeing the measurables and seeing the film. The coach's brain is asking "but can he play?" and the answer should be immediate.

**Film to Origin (Impressed to Respect):** This is where the page shifts from "what he can do" to "who he is." The film showed skill. The origin story shows character-through-action. The transition should feel like: "You saw what he does on Friday night. Here's what happens Monday through Thursday."

**Origin to Character (Respect to Trust):** The origin story builds respect for the work. The character section converts that respect into trust. The transition: "The work ethic is real. But what kind of person puts in that work?"

**Stats to Academics (Confirmation to Relief):** After the stats confirm the film, the academic section removes the last major objection. The transition should feel like a weight lifting: "OK, he can play, he works hard, and he's going to qualify. No red flags."

**The Fit to Contact (Desire to Action):** This is the most important transition on the page. The coach has desire -- they want this kid. Now reduce every possible friction to acting on that desire. The transition should feel like the page is handing the coach a phone and saying "here."

## 5. Behavioral Economics Audit

### Mechanisms Currently Applied

| Mechanism | Where Used | Effectiveness | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|-------|
| Known-ness (K) | Hero, Film, Stats | HIGH | The identity establishment is strong. The coach knows exactly who Jacob is within 3 seconds. |
| Temporal Window (TW) | Origin Story | MEDIUM | The timeline is present but undermined by sentimental copy. The factual progression would be more powerful. |
| Ownership (O) | Character, The Fit | MEDIUM | The word "your" in Section 7 is effective, but the character section's generic traits weaken Ownership. |
| Continuity Thread (CT) | Academics | LOW | The academic section breaks narrative flow rather than continuing it. |
| Forgiving Stakes (FS) | Contact | MEDIUM | The form is low-pressure but the "RECRUIT ME" background contradicts the mechanism. |

### Mechanisms Missing

| Mechanism | Where It Should Be | How to Apply |
|-----------|-------------------|--------------|
| Loss Aversion (LA) | The Fit (Section 7) | "Other programs are evaluating Jacob this spring." The coach who waits might lose. |
| Social Proof | Film section or new section | Camp rankings, coach evaluations, recruiting service assessments. "Rated by [service]. Evaluated at [camp]." |
| Anchoring | Hero section | The 285-lbs-at-15 number should be anchored against the typical freshman weight. "Average Class of 2029 OL: 215 lbs. Jacob: 285 lbs." |
| Reciprocity | Contact section | "Jacob follows your program and has engaged with your content." This triggers the coach to reciprocate attention. |
| Curiosity Gap | Between sections | Each section should leave one question unanswered that the next section answers. Currently, sections are self-contained. |

### The Most Powerful Unused Mechanism: Loss Aversion

Loss aversion is the most potent behavioral trigger in recruiting, and the page does not use it at all.

Coaches do not decide to recruit a player because they want him. They decide because they are afraid another coach will get him first. The recruiting process is fundamentally competitive -- coaches are not just evaluating talent, they are racing against each other.

The page should, at one or two strategic points, remind the coach that they are not the only one reading this page. NOT through desperation ("coaches are blowing up my phone!") but through honest, factual signals:

- "Jacob's NCSA profile has been viewed by [X] programs this month."
- "Currently attending camps in [region]."
- "Building school list for sophomore visits."

These are factual statements that create loss aversion without manipulation.

## 6. Narrative Gaps -- Questions We Are Not Answering

After walking through the entire page from the coach's perspective, here are the questions a coach would have that the page currently does not answer:

### HIGH PRIORITY GAPS

1. **"Who is his trainer/training facility?"** -- Coaches trust known development programs. If Jacob trains somewhere reputable, name it. If he has a position-specific coach, name them.

2. **"What does his head coach say about him?"** -- The most credible voice on a recruiting page is never the recruit or the family. It is the high school coach. One real, attributed quote from Coach [Name] at Pewaukee carries more weight than anything Jacob writes about himself.

3. **"What camps has he attended? What were his evaluations?"** -- Camp performance is how coaches who have not seen Jacob play in person can calibrate his ability. If he attended a Wisconsin camp, an Under Armour camp, or any regional combine, the results should be visible.

4. **"What's his family situation?"** -- Not in a personal sense, but in a recruiting logistics sense. Are the parents engaged? Is there a family contact? Can I talk to his dad? Coaches want to know the family is organized and supportive. A "Family Contact" line in the contact section handles this.

5. **"Does he have any offers or interest from other programs?"** -- This is the most sensitive question and the most powerful one. If Jacob has received interest from other programs (even if it is just camp invites or follow-backs), that validates the coach's interest. "If other programs see it, maybe I should look closer."

### MEDIUM PRIORITY GAPS

6. **"What are his spring/summer plans?"** -- Coaches recruit on a calendar. If Jacob is attending camps, combines, or 7-on-7 events this summer, the coach wants to know so they can evaluate him in person.

7. **"How does he compare to his peers?"** -- The page presents Jacob in isolation. Coaches evaluate in comparison. Any available percentile data, ranking, or camp placement helps.

8. **"What is his injury history?"** -- This is a risk-mitigation question. The page does not need to address this directly unless there is an injury history, but the absence of any mention is normal and fine for a 15-year-old.

9. **"What is his personality like beyond football?"** -- The character section describes football traits. But coaches also want to know: what kind of person is this? What are his interests? What will he be like in a dorm, in a classroom, in the community? A single humanizing detail -- "Jacob volunteers at [X]" or "Interested in studying [X]" -- makes him three-dimensional.

10. **"What does his sophomore season projection look like?"** -- The page is built on freshman stats. By the time a coach reads this in spring/summer 2026, they want to know: what is he doing NOW? Is there updated film? How have his numbers changed?

### LOW PRIORITY GAPS (but worth considering)

11. **"Can I see him in person this spring?"** -- A schedule of upcoming games, camps, or events where the coach could evaluate Jacob live.

12. **"Does he have a preferred position?"** -- DT/OG is a unique combination. Does he prefer one? Where does he project at the college level? A note: "Projects as a 3-technique DT or a pulling guard at the college level" helps the coach mentally place him in their scheme.

## 7. What Makes a Coach Reach Out vs. Close the Tab

### Why Coaches Close the Tab

1. **The measurables do not meet their threshold.** Nothing on the page can overcome this. If a coach needs a 6'5" tackle and Jacob is 6'4", they close the tab. The hero must be honest about measurables so the right coaches self-select in.

2. **The film is not good enough.** Bad film quality (shaky camera, too far away, wrong angle) or underwhelming play kills the page. The film section must be technically excellent: tight angles, clear jersey identification, high-quality clips.

3. **The page feels like a parent project.** This is the biggest risk. If the copy reads like a parent hired a designer and wrote the content, the coach's credibility alarm fires. Coaches want to recruit PLAYERS, not parents. The page must feel like it belongs to Jacob, not to a marketing firm.

4. **The page is too long.** Coaches do not scroll 8 sections. They give it 47 seconds. If the critical information (measurables, film, stats, contact) is not accessible quickly, they leave.

5. **No credible third-party validation.** A page with zero outside voices (no coach quotes, no camp evaluations, no recruiting service ratings) feels like an echo chamber. The family is the only source, and the family is biased.

6. **Broken links, placeholder content, missing video.** The Hudl and NCSA links going to "#" would immediately undermine credibility if a coach clicks them. Every link must work. Every video must load.

### Why Coaches Reach Out

1. **The film shows coachable technique.** Not highlights -- technique. Hand placement, footwork, leverage, finishing. A coach who sees proper technique thinks "I can build on this."

2. **The measurables + age combination is unusual.** 6'4" 285 at 15 is rare. When a coach sees size-at-age that is significantly above average, their projection model kicks in: "If he's this big now, what will he be at 18?"

3. **The page is professional without being overproduced.** Clean design, factual copy, real film, working links. It signals: this family is serious and organized. Recruiting this kid will not be a headache.

4. **There is evidence of coaching and development.** A kid who has been trained by known coaches, at known facilities, with documented progression -- that is lower risk. The coach knows someone has been teaching him properly.

5. **The contact process is easy.** The coach can text, email, or DM. They do not have to fill out a form. The path from "I'm interested" to "I made contact" is 10 seconds.

6. **There is a subtle competitive signal.** The coach senses that other programs are looking. They do not want to be late. This triggers urgency.

## 8. Voice and Tone Strategy

### Current Voice Assessment

The current page has a voice identity crisis. It shifts between three distinct tones:

1. **Cinematic/editorial** -- "What the measurables don't show." "The tape doesn't lie." This is the strongest voice and the one most appropriate for the page. It is confident, sparse, and respects the coach's intelligence.

2. **Parental/sentimental** -- "While other kids played video games, Jacob was in the weight room. Not because someone made him -- because he chose it." This reads like a proud parent wrote it. Coaches can smell this a mile away. It triggers skepticism, not trust.

3. **Marketing/sales** -- "RECRUIT ME." "Five reasons to start the conversation today." "Let's start the conversation." This is the weakest voice. It positions Jacob as a product being sold rather than a player being evaluated.

### Target Voice

The page should sound like a well-made scouting report that was designed beautifully. Not a movie trailer. Not a college essay. Not a sales pitch.

The voice should be:

- **Factual first, emotional second.** Lead with numbers. Let the reader supply the emotion.
- **Understated confidence.** Not "Jacob dominated." Instead: "11 pancake blocks. Freshman season." The fact speaks. The reader is impressed on their own.
- **Third person, not first person.** The page should not say "I" or "my." It should present Jacob as if an objective observer is documenting him. The contact section is the only place where first person is appropriate ("I'm looking for a program where...").
- **Football language, not marketing language.** "Anchors vs. bull rush" not "dominates defenders." "First-step quickness" not "explosive athlete." Speak the coach's language.
- **Sparse.** Every word must earn its place. If a sentence can be cut without losing meaning, cut it.

### Specific Copy Rules

- Never use "grinding" or "grind."
- Never use "built different" or "different breed."
- Never use "DMs are open" or "come get me."
- Never use "dominate" or "destroy" or "pancaked everyone."
- Do use "started," "earned," "competed," "produced."
- Do use specific numbers over adjectives: "265 bench" instead of "strong."
- Do use football-specific language: "hand placement," "leverage," "motor," "pad level."
- Do attribute quotes to real people with real names.
- The page should pass the "would a coach say this?" test. If a coach would not use a phrase in a scouting report, it does not belong on the page.
