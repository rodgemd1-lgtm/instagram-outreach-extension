## THE STORY WE'RE TELLING

### The One-Sentence Version
A 6'4" 285-lb freshman who deadlifts 405, starts on both sides of the ball, plays two games in a day, throws discus for first place, and has logged 730 training sessions since age 12 — and he has three more years to grow.

### The Emotional Core
This is not a story about a big kid who plays football. This is a story about a kid who has been building himself — deliberately, methodically, five days a week for three years — and the results are just starting to show. The freshman season was the first proof point. The trajectory is the real story.

### The Narrative Tension
The tension that makes a coach act is this: **Jacob is undervalued right now.** He's a Class of 2029 recruit with D1 measurables, D1 work ethic, and D1 film — but he's only played one season. The coach who sees this early gets a three-year head start on developing him. The coach who waits will be competing against every other program that discovers him.

### What Makes Jacob's Story Different
Most recruiting pages show a highlight reel and a stat line. Jacob's page shows a **trajectory** — a documented, year-over-year arc of improvement that a coach can project forward. The training at NX Level, the personal trainer, the nutrition focus, the multi-sport explosiveness from track — these are not just facts. They are evidence that this kid's ceiling hasn't been touched.

#### Moment of Truth #2: The Film Test
This is the most important moment on the entire page. The coach is answering one question: "Does this kid have real technique, or is he just big?"

What the coach is looking for in the film:
- **Hand placement** — does he strike inside? Does he control the defender?
- **Footwork** — does he move his feet after contact? Is he balanced?
- **Pad level** — does he play low? Does he use leverage?
- **Motor** — does he finish plays? Does he pursue?
- **Competition level** — is he doing this against real players or against a bad team?

The first play in the reel must be the most technically sound play, not the most flashy. A clean rep with proper technique tells a coach more than a highlight-reel pancake against a weak opponent.

The deadlift number is the new anchor. 405 is unusual for a freshman. It reframes everything else on the page.

#### Moment of Truth #9: The Ownership Trigger
The word "your" does the heavy lifting. "YOUR strength staff gets him." "YOUR program." The coach is no longer evaluating — they're imagining. They're picturing Jacob in their weight room, in their system, wearing their jersey.

#### Moment of Truth #10: The Loss Aversion Trigger
"Interested programs should reach out now" is the first and only competitive signal on the page. It's honest. It's factual. And it tells the coach: you are not the only one reading this.

This is the mechanism that was completely absent from v1. Loss aversion — the fear of missing out on a recruit that another program gets — is the most powerful psychological trigger in recruiting. One sentence is enough. It doesn't need to be aggressive. It just needs to exist.

#### Moment of Truth #11: "What He's Looking For"
This is a strategic inversion. Instead of selling Jacob TO the coach, this block lets the coach evaluate whether THEY are a fit for Jacob. This triggers a different kind of Ownership — the coach thinks: "We have a strong OL tradition. We develop players. He'd love our program."

The coach is now selling themselves to Jacob in their own head. That's the moment they decide to reach out.

- Added "The Window" — creates honest urgency without desperation
- The 405 deadlift and multi-sport explosiveness are referenced here because this is where they serve the narrative: as proof of development potential, not just current ability

## COPY RULES — VOICE AND TONE

### The Page Should Sound Like:
A well-designed scouting report. Factual. Confident. Sparse.
The design is premium. The words are functional.

### Rules:
1. Numbers before adjectives. "405 deadlift" not "incredibly strong."
2. Football language. "Anchors vs. bull rush" not "dominates defenders."
3. Third person. No "I" or "my" until the contact section.
4. No parent-speak. Remove: "chose to," "not because someone made him," "passion."
5. No recruiting cliches. Remove: "built different," "grind," "DMs are open."
6. Use: "started," "earned," "produced," "logged," "placed first."
7. Every claim backed by a fact. No unsupported adjectives.
8. The coach quote is real, attributed, and specific.
9. All links work. All videos load. All numbers are accurate.

### The "Would a Coach Say This?" Test
Read every line on the page and ask: would a college football coach use this phrase in a scouting report? If not, rewrite it until they would.
